    0 → Competent: Shatterplay Building Blocks

    Frost Mage / Shadow Priest / Resto Shaman, from your Draenei SPriest seat.

    The 3 Phases of Every Game

    1
    Open — first 10-15s. Mage waits for water elemental + shatter setup, you apply MF/VT and hold your CC for the read, shaman watches for the opposing kill target. Nobody dies here on a clean game — this is where most of your logged losses actually happened (caught off guard, split up, own shaman trained).
    2
    Mid-game — pressure and baiting. Force out a defensive CD (Ice Block, Cloak, PS, Divine Shield) with a soft commit, then flip to whoever it exposes. This is where your kill-target flexibility (documented across almost every matchup) actually pays off.
    3
    Close — CDs are down, someone is exposed. Full commit, chain your CC on the healer/off-target so they can't peel, finish in order.
    Your job as Shadowpriest›
    • You have no personal defensive cooldown beyond Fade/Dispersion (later tier) and your trinket — this is exactly why you're a frequent kill target and why positioning beats reacting.
    • Your core toolkit: Mind Flay for pressure + silence-on-cast, Vampiric Touch/Shadow Word: Pain for sustained pressure that doesn't require you to stand and cast, Psychic Scream for a full-team fear (positioning-dependent, can save yourself or peel for your shaman), Dispel Magic for offensive dispel value (see class notes — Blessing of Sacrifice, Ice Barrier-type shields, HoTs).
    • Mind Flay's channel makes you vulnerable — don't commit to a full channel blind, especially if their kill target (you) hasn't been confirmed safe.
    • You are also your team's mobile CC — Psychic Scream and Silence are both position/timing tools, not just damage tools. Use them to buy your shaman time, not only to secure kills.
    Talent & stat priorities (general TBC convention)›
    • Shadow Priest PvP builds conventionally prioritize Shadow Focus, Improved Shadow Word: Pain, Silence, and Psychic Horror/Vampiric Embrace as available — exact point allocation shifts by patch, so cross-check current talent calculators before finalizing.
    • Frost Mage runs deep Frost for the shatter combo and CC uptime (Improved Frost Nova, Ice Block/Cold Snap access, Shatter itself).
    • Resto Shaman runs deep Restoration for Nature's Swiftness, Earth Shield efficiency, and Tidal Focus/mana tools — this spec is more cast-reliant than other TBC healers, which is exactly why protecting your shaman early matters so much.
    • Resilience is your #1 PvP stat once you have your core damage/healing talents — it reduces incoming damage and CC duration on you.
    Addon setup›
    • Gladius (or Gladdy) — arena unit frames with CC/diminishing-return tracking. Non-negotiable for arena.
    • OmniCC / OmniBar — cooldown tracking for enemy trinkets, defensives, and CDs (Ice Block, Bestial Wrath, Death Wish, etc.) — referenced constantly in the how-to-kill breakdowns you already have.
    • A DR (diminishing returns) tracker — critical for knowing when your CC chain is actually going to land vs. resist/half-duration.
    Beginner mistakes (from your own loss data)›
    • Getting caught off guard in the opener. Multiple logged losses trace directly to this — slow down and confirm positioning before committing, especially against hunters and rogues.
    • Own shaman getting isolated and killed first. The mirror-match loss is the clearest example. If your shaman is under pressure in the first 10s, that takes priority over your own damage.
    • Committing into a defensive CD instead of baiting it first. Several losses came from dumping cooldowns into Ice Block/PS rather than forcing it out with a soft engage first.
    • Not updating the kill target when the read changes. Your wins consistently show target flips (mage iceblocks → go rogue instead); losses often show tunneling the original plan too long.
    How to keep improving from here›
    • Log every ranked game you play in the Log tab, win or lose — your own games are more valuable data than any VOD, since they reflect your actual execution.
    • Go through your 5 queued videos when you get a chance — prioritize any matchup you've only logged once, or haven't seen at all yet, over ones you already have 3+ games on.
    • After every loss, ask one question before queuing again: was it a read problem (wrong target, wrong timing) or an execution problem (missed a kick, mispositioned)? Note it in that game's log entry — patterns will show up in the matchup notes over time.
    • Once you're comfortable with your top 4-5 most common matchups, start deliberately reviewing the ones you're weakest against (lowest win rate in the Matchups tab) rather than the ones you already win.
